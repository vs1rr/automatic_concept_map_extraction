William McKinley was the 25th President of the United States, serving from 1897 until his assassination in 1901.
He led the nation to victory in the Spanish-American War, raised protective tariffs, and maintained the gold standard.
McKinley's presidency marked the beginning of Republican Party dominance for over a third of a century.
He was the last president to have served in the Civil War and promoted economic growth through tariffs and the gold standard.
McKinley was assassinated by an anarchist in September 1901 and was succeeded by Vice President Theodore Roosevelt.
His assassination led to Roosevelt taking office and continuing McKinley's political agenda.
The assassin, Leon Czolgosz, was found guilty and executed.
McKinley's death marked the end of his presidency, which was characterized by economic growth, victory in the Spanish-American War, and a focus on tariffs and the gold standard.
His assassination had a significant impact on the nation and led to the presidency of Theodore Roosevelt.
