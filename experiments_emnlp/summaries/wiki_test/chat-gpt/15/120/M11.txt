Sir John Betjeman was an English poet, writer, and broadcaster who served as Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom from 1972 until his death in 1984.
He was a passionate defender of Victorian architecture and a founding member of the Victorian Society.
Betjeman was born in London in 1906 and attended Oxford University, where he struggled academically but excelled socially.
He began his career as a journalist and worked as a film critic before becoming a full-time assistant editor at the Architectural Review.
Betjeman's poetry is known for its humor and accessibility, often focusing on everyday life and the beauty of the ordinary.
He was a practicing Anglican and his religious beliefs influenced his work.
Betjeman was also a strong advocate for the preservation of historic buildings and landmarks, leading campaigns to save several architectural treasures.
He was honored with numerous awards and accolades during his lifetime, and his legacy continues to be celebrated through various events and memorials.
Betjeman's work remains popular and influential, reflecting his unique perspective on British culture and society.
