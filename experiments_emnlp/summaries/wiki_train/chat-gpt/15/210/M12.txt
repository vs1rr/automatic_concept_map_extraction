The Third Intermediate Period in Egypt (1070-712 B.C.) was a time of politically divided rule and diffused power, lasting for approximately 350 years after the death of Ramesses XI.
The period can be divided into three stages, with Dynasty 21 and Dynasty 22 ruling from different centers of power.
The period saw the rise of a powerful family of Libyan descent ruling in the eastern Delta, with pharaohs like Sheshonq I playing significant roles.
The weakening of centralized royal authority led to the emergence of the temple network as a dominant sphere for political aspirations, social identification, and artistic production.
The temple precincts became favored burial sites, and innovative bronze and precious temple statuary flourished.
