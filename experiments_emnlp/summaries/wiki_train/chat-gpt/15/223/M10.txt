Justinian I, born around 482 CE in Illyria, was one of the most important late Roman and Byzantine emperors.
He reigned from 527 to 565 CE and is known for his military campaigns to retake Africa from the Vandals and Italy from the Goths.
He also ordered the rebuilding of the Hagia Sophia church and completed the Codex Iustinianus, a compilation of Roman laws.
Justinian's early life is not well-documented, but he was brought to Constantinople by his uncle Emperor Justin I for education.
He married Theodora, a woman from a poor background, in 525 CE.
Justinian initiated an enormous military enterprise to take Italy, Sicily, and Africa, starting with the Iberian War against the Sassanian Empire.
He successfully reconquered Africa from the Vandals and Italy from the Goths, although the Gothic War continued with the rise of Totila.
Justinian's reign was marked by civil discontent, including the Nika riots in 532 CE.
Procopius of Caesarea recorded Justinian's life in two works, praising his military campaigns and building projects but also criticizing his regime in the Anecdota.
