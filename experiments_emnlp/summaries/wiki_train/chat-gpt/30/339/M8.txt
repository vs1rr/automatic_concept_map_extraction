James Madison was born on March 16, 1751, in Virginia, the eldest son of a wealthy landowner.
He attended the College of New Jersey at Princeton, where he was exposed to the Scottish Enlightenment and graduated in 1771.
Madison entered politics during the imperial crisis, contributing to the independence movement in Virginia.
He served in the Virginia House of Delegates and the Continental Congress, where he advocated for a stronger central government.
In 1787, Madison played a key role in drafting the Constitution at the Constitutional Convention.
He worked to get the Constitution ratified and later served in the U.S. House of Representatives, where he played a crucial role in the passage of the Bill of Rights.
Madison's time in Congress was marked by disagreements with Alexander Hamilton over financial policies and the formation of the Federalist and Republican parties.
He retired from Congress in 1797 but continued to be involved in national issues, particularly in opposition to the Alien and Sedition Acts.
Madison served as Secretary of State under Thomas Jefferson and succeeded him as President in 1808.
His presidency was marked by the War of 1812, which ended with the Treaty of Ghent in 1814.
Madison's second term focused on domestic affairs, including the establishment of the Second Bank of the United States.
He retired to his plantation in 1817 and remained active in public life until his death in 1836.
