James Madison was born on March 16, 1751 in Virginia to James Madison Sr. and Nelly Conway Madison.
He received his education at various schools and Princeton University, where he studied a variety of subjects.
Madison became involved in politics during the American Revolution, serving in various roles in Virginia and the Continental Congress.
He played a key role in the Constitutional Convention of 1787, where he proposed the Virginia Plan that influenced the final Constitution.
Madison also wrote The Federalist essays to promote the new Constitution.
He served in Congress and as Secretary of State under Thomas Jefferson before becoming the fourth President of the United States in 1809.
During his presidency, Madison faced challenges such as the War of 1812 against Great Britain.
Despite initial criticism, Madison's leadership during the war led to a sense of nationalism and he left office with high regard.
After retiring from the presidency, Madison spent his final years organizing his papers and receiving visitors at Montpelier.
He died on June 28, 1836.
Madison's contributions to the founding of the United States and his dedication to public service were remembered by his contemporaries, who hoped that his life would inspire future patriots.
