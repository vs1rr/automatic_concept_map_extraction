The Book of Kells is a highly revered manuscript containing 678 illustrated pages made of calf-skin or vellum.
It is believed to have been written in a Columban Monastery on the island of Iona in Scotland and colored using natural dyes by monks.
The ink used in the book has not faded in over 1000 years.
The four symbols in the book, often thought to represent the Apostles, actually represent the Twelve Tribes of Israel.
The Irish people are said to be mostly descended from the Tribe of Dan, who arrived in Ireland under various names and at different times.
The Tuatha de Danaan, considered the Tribe of Dan, were followed by further immigrations of Danites under different names such as the Milesians and Dan-ish Vikings.
The Eagle, one of the symbols in the Book of Kells, is the tribal standard of Dan, who is believed to have discovered America before Columbus.
The four tribal standards mentioned in the Book of Kells are also referenced in the Apocalypse/Revelation, where they are associated with the four beasts before the Throne.
The emblems are said to represent the tribes of Israel to whom the Gospels were to be preached.
James, the brother of Jesus, later wrote to all twelve tribes of Israel in the New Testament.
The Book of Kells holds significant historical and religious importance, reflecting the connection between the Irish people and the tribes of Israel.
