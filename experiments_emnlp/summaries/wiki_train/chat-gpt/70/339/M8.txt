James Madison was born on March 16, 1751, in Virginia, the eldest son of a wealthy landowner.
He attended the College of New Jersey at Princeton, where he was exposed to the ideas of the Scottish Enlightenment.
After graduating in 1771, Madison entered a career in politics, serving in the Virginia Convention of 1776 and the Virginia House of Delegates.
He was elected to the Continental Congress in 1779 and played a key role in shaping the Constitution at the Constitutional Convention in 1787.
Madison went on to serve in the U.S. House of Representatives, where he played a crucial role in the passage of the Bill of Rights.
He later served as Secretary of State under Thomas Jefferson and was elected as the fourth President of the United States in 1808.
During his presidency, Madison faced challenges such as the War of 1812, which ended with the Treaty of Ghent in 1814.
After retiring from office in 1817, Madison remained active in public life, advocating for issues such as scientific farming and education.
He passed away on June 28, 1836, leaving behind a legacy as one of the Founding Fathers of the United States.
