The British Empire originated in the late 16th century with private commercial ventures chartered by the crown, leading to the establishment of fortified trading posts in India, the East Indies, Newfoundland, and Hudson Bay.
The demand for sugar and tobacco in Europe led to the growth of plantations in the Caribbean and North America, attracting European settlers.
The need for cheap labor resulted in the growth of the African slave trade, with chartered companies securing posts on the African coast.
The Navigation Acts were passed to increase British wealth and naval strength by restricting colonial trade to British ships.
In the late 17th and early 18th centuries, the weakening of the Spanish and Dutch empires allowed for British encroachment on their territories, while Anglo-French rivalry grew in India, Canada, and Africa.
The British government attempted to assert greater control over the expanding empire, resulting in friction with colonial assemblies.
The British East India Company engaged in a military and commercial rivalry with its French counterpart in India, ultimately emerging victorious.
The Treaty of Paris in 1763 firmly established British control in India and Canada, but financial difficulties with the American colonies led to the American Revolution, marking the end of the first British Empire.
