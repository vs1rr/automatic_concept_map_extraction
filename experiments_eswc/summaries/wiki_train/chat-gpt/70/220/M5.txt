The defeat of Napoleon in 1815 marked the end of a prolonged global conflict that had been ongoing since the 1740s.
Despite the peace achieved at the Vienna Congress, there were still fears of renewed war.
Britain emerged as a dominant imperial power among Europe's Great Powers, with a strong navy securing global supremacy at sea.
The Act of Union with Ireland in 1801 had recently consolidated the home islands, but cultural distinctions between England, Scotland, Wales, and Ireland remained.
Britain's empire consisted of three main elements: the colonies of white settlement in North America, the complex eastern empire in India, and the historic sugar colonies in the Caribbean.
British expansion in India, under Governors-General Wellesley and Hastings, progressively overrode Indian independence by 1820.
The Caribbean colonies faced threats from internal slave revolts and external enemies, but Britain made territorial gains in Trinidad and South America.
In West Africa, British possessions were limited to Sierra Leone and trading posts.
The growth of Britain's empire was shaped by global warfare, personal ambitions, and encounters with diverse local societies.
The idea of a systematic search for a new empire to replace the lost American colonies is not supported by evidence.
The value of the western empire to Britain remained significant, overshadowing Asian trade until the 1840s.
The expansion of the British Empire was not a coherent plan but a result of various factors and encounters with different societies.
Professor Andrew Porter, an expert in imperial history, has written extensively on the British Empire and is currently working on a book about Protestant missions and empire.
