The Book of Kells is a highly revered manuscript containing 678 illustrated pages made of calf-skin or vellum.
It is believed to have been written in the Columban Monastery's scriptorum on the island of Iona in Scotland and colored using natural dyes by monks.
The ink used in the book has not faded in over 1000 years.
While many believe that the four symbols in the book represent the four Apostles, they actually represent the Twelve Tribes of Israel, with each symbol corresponding to a specific tribe.
The Irish people are said to be mostly descended from the Tribe of Dan, one of Jacob/Israel's twelve sons.
The Tuatha de Danaan, an ancient Irish tribe, are believed to be descendants of the Tribe of Dan.
The Book of Kells also features the four tribal standards mentioned in the Apocalypse/Revelation, further connecting it to the tribes of Israel.
The book's inclusion of these symbols is seen as a representation of the Gospels being preached to the tribes of Israel.
James, the brother of Jesus, later wrote to all twelve tribes of Israel in his letter in the New Testament.
The Book of Kells holds significant historical and religious importance, showcasing the connection between the Irish people and the tribes of Israel.
