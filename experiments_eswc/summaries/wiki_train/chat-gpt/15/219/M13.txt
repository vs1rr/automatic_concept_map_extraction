Mark Oliphant was a physicist born in Australia in 1901, known for his work on splitting the atom and his involvement in the development of the atom bomb during World War II.
Despite his initial role in the bomb's creation, Oliphant later became a vocal advocate for peace and humanitarianism, speaking out against the use of nuclear weapons.
He was appalled by the devastation caused by the bombs dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki in 1945.
Oliphant's anti-nuclear weapons stance led to him being excluded from certain scientific experiments involving nuclear power.
He returned to Australia after the war, where he continued his scientific work and became involved in politics, serving as the state governor of South Australia from 1971 to 1976.
Oliphant was knighted in 1959 and received numerous honors for his contributions to science and society.
He passed away in 2000 at the age of 98.
Despite his involvement in the creation of the atom bomb, Oliphant will be remembered for his efforts to promote peace and his opposition to the further development and use of nuclear weapons.
His legacy lives on through various locations and institutions named in his honor, recognizing his significant contributions to science and his unwavering commitment to humanitarian causes.
