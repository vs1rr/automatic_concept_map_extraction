The Pendle witches were a group of twelve people accused of witchcraft in the 17th century, with only one being found not guilty.
The trial was well-documented in an official publication by Thomas Potts.
The background to the events of the trials involved two rival families, the Demdike and Chattox families, who had been known as witches for years.
The accusations stemmed from a feud between the families and incidents of alleged witchcraft.
The trials were influenced by King James
I's interest in witchcraft and the general fear and fascination with the practice at the time.
The trials resulted in the execution of ten people, accounting for 2% of all witches executed in England.
The story of the Pendle witches remains a well-known legend due to the unusual circumstances and the extent of the accusations made.
The trials were a reflection of the social unrest and fear of witchcraft in the country at the time, making it one of the most notorious witch trials in history.
