The Borobudur Temple Compounds in central Java is a famous Buddhist temple dating back to the 8th and 9th centuries.
It consists of a pyramidal base with five terraces, circular platforms, and a monumental stupa.
The walls are adorned with intricate reliefs, and there are 72 stupas around the platforms, each containing a Buddha statue.
The temple represents the Buddhist concept of the universe divided into three spheres.
It is a blend of ancestor worship and the pursuit of Nirvana.
The temple, along with two smaller temples, represents the stages of attaining Nirvana.
The site was used as a Buddhist temple until the 15th century when it was abandoned.
It was restored in the 20th century and is now a Buddhist archaeological site.
The temple is a masterpiece of Buddhist architecture and art, influencing Indonesian architecture for centuries.
The property faces threats from development, tourism, and natural disasters like volcanic eruptions.
The temple has been reconstructed using original materials and is protected under Indonesian cultural heritage laws.
The management of the site involves various government bodies and community development programs.
Monitoring programs are in place to address deterioration and damage to the temple.
A risk preparedness plan is being formulated to protect the property from potential threats.
The temple is a UNESCO World Heritage site and a significant pilgrimage site for Buddhists.
The management of the site aims to balance conservation efforts with tourism and commercial activities to preserve its authenticity and outstanding universal value.
