Jesus College, Oxford is one of the colleges of the University of Oxford, founded in 1571 by Queen Elizabeth I. Initially established for the education of clergy, the college now offers a broad range of secular subjects.
The oldest buildings date back to the 16th and early 17th centuries, with additional quadrangles added in the 17th and 20th centuries.
The college faced disruptions during the English Civil War but was stabilized financially by Leoline Jenkins in the 17th century.
In the 19th century, academic standards declined, leading to reforms that opened up fellowships and scholarships to a wider range of students.
Women were first admitted in 1974, and the college now has about 475 students, with Sir Nigel Shadbolt serving as the Principal.
Notable alumni include Harold Wilson, T.E. Lawrence, and Norman Washington Manley.
The college has a strong Welsh connection, with a significant number of Welsh students and scholars associated with the institution.
The college's coat of arms features three stags on a green background, and its silverware collection includes a silver-gilt punch bowl presented by Sir Watkin Williams-Wynn in 1732.
The college's rowing club, Jesus College Boat Club, has a long history and has produced successful rowers who have competed in the Boat Race and the Women's Boat Race.
The college also has a strong tradition of student life, with various extracurricular activities and events, including the Turl Street Arts Festival and St. David's Day celebrations.
